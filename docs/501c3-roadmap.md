# Harris-Nelson Family Foundation — 501(c)(3) Startup Roadmap

Goal: within the next few months, create a nonprofit foundation that gives our
scholarship fund, land-back work, and reunion legacy a permanent, tax-exempt
legal home.

> This is a practical checklist, not legal advice. Before filing, have a
> nonprofit attorney or a service like your state's nonprofit association
> review the paperwork — many offer free/cheap help for small foundations.

## Phase 1 — Organize (Month 1)
- [ ] **Pick the legal name** (e.g., "Harris-Nelson Family Foundation") and check
      availability on the state's business registry
- [ ] **Elect the founding board** at a family business meeting (Robert's Rules:
      nominations → second → vote). Recommended:
      - Board Chair, Vice Chair, Board Secretary, Board Treasurer
      - 3–5 at-large directors — ideally one per generation
      - Note: IRS prefers boards where a majority are not closely related **by
        household/financial interest**; with a family foundation, document how
        decisions avoid private benefit. An attorney can advise on composition.
- [ ] **Draft bylaws** — meetings, elections, terms, quorum, how money is approved
- [ ] **Adopt a conflict-of-interest policy** (IRS provides sample language in the
      Form 1023 instructions)
- [ ] **Define the charitable purposes** in writing. Ours:
      1. Scholarships for descendants of our ancestors (objective, published criteria)
      2. Historical/genealogical research and preservation, incl. land-records research
      3. Educational programming at family gatherings

## Phase 2 — Incorporate (Month 1–2)
- [ ] **File Articles of Incorporation** as a nonprofit corporation with the state
      (must include IRS-required purpose and dissolution clauses — use the IRS
      sample language)
- [ ] **Get an EIN** — free, 10 minutes, at IRS.gov (never pay a third party for this)
- [ ] **Hold the first board meeting** — adopt bylaws, elect officers, authorize the
      bank account and the 1023 filing; Secretary keeps minutes

## Phase 3 — Federal tax exemption (Month 2–3)
- [ ] **Form 1023-EZ** ($275, online, ~2–4 week turnaround) if projected annual gross
      receipts ≤ $50,000 and assets ≤ $250,000 — likely our situation at the start
- [ ] Otherwise **Form 1023** (full version, $600, several months)
- [ ] Decide **public charity vs. private foundation** status — a family scholarship
      fund that fundraises broadly usually seeks **public charity** status;
      scholarship procedures may need IRS approval (esp. if classified as a private
      foundation). Flag this specifically for the attorney.

## Phase 4 — State & operations (Month 3+)
- [ ] **State charitable solicitation registration** (required in most states before
      publicly fundraising)
- [ ] **Open the bank account** (needs EIN + articles + board resolution)
- [ ] **Adopt the first annual budget**; set the fundraising split between
      scholarship fund, land-back retainer, and reunion seed money
- [ ] **Set up bookkeeping** and a receipts process (Financial Secretary model
      carries over) — 501(c)(3)s file an annual IRS Form 990-N/990-EZ
- [ ] **Publish everything to the family** — bylaws, minutes, budgets on
      hnfamilyreunion.com

## How the foundation relates to the reunion
- The **reunion organization** (officers + committees) keeps running the reunion.
- The **foundation** is a separate legal entity with its own board and votes; it
  holds the charitable money (scholarships, land fund) so donations are
  tax-deductible and protected.
- Reunion officers may serve on the foundation board, but the two bodies vote
  separately and keep separate books.

## Scholarship program guardrails (IRS-safe)
- Published, objective eligibility criteria (descendant + enrolled/enrolling in an
  accredited program)
- A selection committee (Philanthropy & Fundraising) with documented scoring
- Awards paid to the school where possible; keep records of each award
